Lesson 1: The Writer's Pact

Program: The Writer's Apprenticeship: A Two-Year, Self-Paced MFA

Position: Year 1, Semester 1, Week 1

Estimated Time Commitment: 3-4 hours

Prerequisites: None

1. Learning Objectives

  • Design and commit to a sustainable weekly writing schedule based on your personal goals and intentions.
  • Differentiate between "showing" (conveying information through sensory detail and action) and "telling" (conveying information through exposition and summary).
  • Analyze the difference between active and passive voice and convert passive sentences into their more vigorous active forms.
  • Produce a 1,000-word descriptive scene that utilizes concrete sensory details and the active voice to create an immersive experience for the reader.

2. Written Lecture

The Myth of the Muse and the Power of the Pact

Let us begin by dismantling the most destructive myth in the creative arts: the myth of the muse. This is the romantic notion of the writer as a passive vessel for divine inspiration, waiting patiently for a lightning bolt of genius to strike. When it does, the story pours out, fully formed and perfect.

This is a fantasy. As Stephen King notes in On Writing, "Don't wait for the muse... Your job is to make sure the muse knows where you're going to be every day from nine 'til noon or seven 'til three." Waiting for the muse is an excuse for not writing.

The truth is that writing is a job. It is a blue-collar trade that requires you to show up, punch the clock, and put in the hours, whether you feel "inspired" or not. The writers whose work you admire did not produce it by waiting. They produced it through discipline, routine, and a workmanlike commitment to their craft.

Part I: Forging the Pact - Your Writing Routine

Consistency is vastly more productive than intensity. Writing for one hour every day is infinitely more valuable than writing for eight hours one day a month. Your commitment begins here, with a pact that has three components:

  • Time and Place: Designate a specific, non-negotiable time and place for your writing. When you repeatedly show up to the same spot at the same time, you are training your brain. You are telling it, "This is where we work." The space itself becomes a creative trigger.
  • Measurable Goals: Your goal should be manageable and measurable. Many writers prefer a time-based goal (e.g., "I will write for one hour") over a word-count goal. A time-based goal measures effort, which you can always control, rather than output, which can fluctuate wildly.
  • The Sanctity of the Act: Protect your writing time ruthlessly. Turn off your phone. Disconnect the internet. It is a meeting with your most important collaborator: your own creative mind.

Part II: The Cornerstone of Craft - Show, Don't Tell

"Telling" is stating a fact or an emotion directly. It is a summary. "Showing" is the translation of these abstract statements into concrete, sensory experiences that the reader can see, hear, feel, taste, and smell. It is the creation of a scene.

Why is showing so much more effective? Because it does not report an experience to the reader; it invites the reader to live inside the experience. When you tell the reader that Susan is angry, you ask them to trust your summary. When you show her white-knuckled grip, you allow the reader to deduce her anger for themselves.

Part III: The Engine of Prose - The Active Voice

If "Show, Don't Tell" is the philosophy, the active voice is the engine that drives it. As laid out in The Elements of Style, "The active voice is usually more direct and vigorous than the passive."

  • Active Voice: The subject of the sentence performs the action. ("The writer drafted the chapter.")
  • Passive Voice: The subject of the sentence receives the action. ("The chapter was drafted by the writer.")

The active sentence is concise and energetic. The passive sentence is wordy, indirect, and weak. As you edit your work, you must become a hunter of the passive voice.

3. Reading Assignment

  • Primary Reading: The short story "Hills Like White Elephants" by Ernest Hemingway.
  • Secondary Reading: The Elements of Style by William Strunk Jr. & E.B. White. Read Rule 14, "Use the active voice," and Rule 16, "Use definite, specific, concrete language."
  • Reading Journal Prompt: In 300 words, analyze the relationship between the active voice and the "Show, Don't Tell" principle in "Hills Like White Elephants." How do these two craft elements work together to create the story's powerful subtext?

4. Writing Assignment

  • Warm-up Exercise (10 minutes): Choose a single object in your immediate vicinity. Write one paragraph describing it, focusing on engaging at least four of the five senses. Do not state what the object is.
  • Primary Exercise (1,000 words): Write a 1,000-word scene describing a character performing a simple, mundane task while feeling a powerful, unstated emotion. You are forbidden from naming the emotion.

    • Example: "The Folding"

    Margaret pulls the warm laundry from the dryer, the metal drum still clicking as it cools. Steam rises from the cotton shirts and towels, carrying the scent of lavender fabric softener—the same brand she has bought for twenty-three years. She dumps the clothes onto the bed with more force than necessary, watching as David's white undershirts scatter across the comforter like fallen soldiers.

    She picks up his favorite blue polo first, the one with the tiny hole near the left shoulder seam. Her fingers find the worn spot automatically, tracing the frayed threads she has offered to mend a dozen times. "It gives it character," he always says, that crooked smile spreading across his face. She shakes the shirt twice, sharp snaps that echo in the bedroom, then smooths it against her chest.

    The collar needs straightening. She runs her thumb along the edge, pressing out phantom wrinkles that exist only in her mind. The fabric feels different today—rougher somehow, as if the fibers themselves resist her touch. She folds the sleeves inward with mechanical precision, creating perfect lines that bisect the chest. Left sleeve, right sleeve, fold in half. The same motions she has performed thousands of times, yet her hands tremble slightly as she places the shirt in David's drawer.

    A pair of his jeans comes next, heavy denim that still holds the shape of his legs. She grips the waistband and gives them a violent shake, watching the legs dance in the air like a marionette. The sound of the metal zipper striking the bed frame makes her flinch. She spreads the jeans flat, running her palms down the length to smooth them, her movements growing more aggressive with each pass.

    The creases refuse to cooperate. She presses harder, her knuckles white against the dark fabric, trying to force the stubborn denim into submission. A bead of sweat forms at her temple despite the air conditioning that hums steadily in the background. She wipes it away with the back of her hand and continues folding, her breathing shallow and quick.

    David's socks come in pairs, but she separates them anyway, rolling each one individually before nestling them together again. The ritual takes longer this way, but she needs the extra time, needs the deliberate motions to keep her thoughts from wandering to the manila envelope that waits on the kitchen counter. The one with the law firm's return address in the upper left corner.

    She moves to her own clothes now, a floral blouse she wore to her sister's birthday party last month. The memory surfaces unbidden—how David had complimented the way the blue brought out her eyes, how he had held her hand during the drive home, his thumb stroking her knuckles in that absent way that meant contentment. She crumples the blouse in her fists, then immediately smooths it out, horrified by her loss of control.

    The wrinkles she has created stare back at her like accusations. She shakes the blouse again, harder this time, then begins the folding process with exaggerated care. Each movement becomes a meditation, a desperate attempt to maintain order in a world that tilts dangerously on its axis. She aligns the shoulders, smooths the sleeves, creates a perfect rectangle that she places in her drawer with the reverence of a sacred offering.

    Her underwear requires no folding, just sorting. She separates the practical cotton briefs from the silky pieces she bought years ago in a moment of optimism, pieces that remain unworn, tags still attached. She handles them roughly now, shoving them toward the back of the drawer where they belong, where they have always belonged.

    A red sock appears in the pile—David's athletic sock from his morning runs. She holds it up to the light, studying the hole in the heel that she keeps meaning to darn. The edges have frayed further since she last examined it, the cotton threads creating a small crater that reveals her palm beneath. She makes a mental note to add it to the mending basket, then immediately discards the thought. What point would there be in fixing it now?

    She tosses the sock toward David's drawer but it falls short, landing on the carpet like a deflated balloon. She leaves it there.

    The remaining clothes blur together—t-shirts and tank tops, pajama pants and workout shorts. Her hands move without conscious direction, folding and sorting with the efficiency of long practice. But each completed garment feels like an ending, each emptied section of the bed bringing her closer to the moment when she will have no choice but to leave this room, walk down the hallway, and return to that envelope.

    The last item waits at the bottom of the pile—David's gray sweater, the one she bought him for Christmas three years ago. He claimed it made him look distinguished, would strike poses in front of the bedroom mirror until she collapsed in laughter. The wool feels soft between her fingers, expensive and well-made, designed to last for decades.

    She brings it to her face and inhales deeply, searching for traces of his cologne, his skin, his presence. But the commercial laundry detergent has erased all evidence of him, leaving only the generic scent of cleanliness. She holds the sweater against her chest and sits heavily on the edge of the bed, the empty laundry basket at her feet, the sorted clothes arranged in neat piles around her.

    The house settles around her with small creaks and sighs, the same sounds it has made for fifteen years. Through the window, she can see Mrs. Chen watering her roses, the afternoon sun casting long shadows across the manicured lawns. Everything appears normal, unchanged, as if the world has not shifted fundamentally in the past three hours.

    She stands abruptly and walks to David's closet, still clutching the sweater. His suits hang in a perfect row, organized by color and season. She pushes the hangers aside and hangs the sweater in the center, arranging it carefully so the shoulders align with the wooden frame.

    Then she closes the closet door and turns toward the hallway, where the envelope waits with its terrible, necessary words.

  • Revision Task: After completing your draft, identify any sentences in the passive voice and rewrite them. Find at least three places where you "told" the reader something and replace it with a concrete, sensory detail that "shows" it instead.
  • Reflection Component (200 words): What was the most challenging part of this exercise? Was it avoiding the passive voice or showing the emotion without naming it? Which sensory detail in your final draft do you feel is most effective, and why?

5. AI Integration Component

  • AI Brainstorming Prompt: Prompt: "I am writing a scene about a character who is secretly grieving while performing the task of cooking a meal. Generate a list of 20 concrete, sensory details (sights, sounds, smells, tastes, textures) that could show this internal grief without ever using words like 'sad,' 'grief,' or 'loss.'"
  • AI Editing Exercise: Prompt: "Analyze the following text. Identify every sentence written in the passive voice and provide a more direct, active-voice alternative for each one." Paste your draft below this prompt.
  • AI Critique & Ethical Considerations: Review the AI's suggestions. Are the sensory details it provided original, or are they clichés? Are the active-voice alternatives stronger? The AI is a tool for brainstorming and technical checks. It is not the author. Your responsibility is to evaluate, select, and integrate ideas, then rewrite them in your own unique voice.

6. Community Component

Peer Exchange: Find an accountability partner. Share your 1,000-word scene with them. Do not tell them what emotion you were trying to convey. Ask them one question: "After reading this, what do you believe the character is feeling, and what specific detail or action made you feel that?"

AI Writers' Group Chat: Attach your scene draft to the Perfect Writers' Group and ask the same question.

7. Self-Assessment Tools

  • Progress Checklist: Have you designed a weekly writing schedule? Completed all readings and exercises? Saved your scene to your Semester 1 Portfolio?
  • Skills Rubric: On a scale of Developing to Proficient to Advanced, how well did you use sensory details to imply emotion? How successfully did you eliminate passive voice?

8. Extension Activities (Optional)

  • For Advanced Students: Rewrite your 1,000-word scene from the point of view of a second character who is observing the first.
  • For Struggling Students: Reduce the scope. Write a single, 300-word paragraph describing a character entering a room, focusing only on "showing" their emotion.

9. Key Takeaways & Next Steps

  • Essential Concepts: Discipline, not inspiration, is the foundation of a writing life. Showing creates an immersive experience; telling creates distance. The active voice is the engine of strong, direct, and energetic prose.
  • Preview: In Lesson 2, we will build on these fundamentals to explore the engine of all stories: The Engine of Story: Goal, Motivation, and Conflict.

10. Resources

  • Book: Bird by Bird: Some Instructions on Writing and Life by Anne Lamott. (An excellent and humane guide to building a writing routine).
  • Online Tool: The Hemingway App. (A free online editor that highlights passive voice, adverbs, and complex sentences, helping you train your eye).
